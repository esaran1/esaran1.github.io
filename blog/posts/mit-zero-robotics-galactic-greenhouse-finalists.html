MIT Zero Robotics: Galactic Greenhouse — How We Became Finalists

January 2026 · 6 min read

This year my team competed in MIT Zero Robotics, and the game was Galactic Greenhouse. We ended up becoming finalists, which still feels a little unreal.

This post is a quick recap: what the challenge felt like, what our iteration loop looked like, and the specific things we did that made our bot more reliable over time.

What is Zero Robotics?

Zero Robotics is a programming competition with “space” constraints: limited time, noisy sensing, tight control, and game objectives that reward consistency. The fun part is that it pushes you to think like a systems engineer: behavior, edge cases, testing, and scoring all matter.

In Galactic Greenhouse, we had to balance doing the “obvious” scoring tasks with making decisions that held up under uncertainty and collision-like situations.

How we approached Galactic Greenhouse

  • Reliability first: a slightly lower-scoring strategy that never crashes beats a higher-scoring one that fails 1/10 runs.
  • Simple state machine: clear phases with explicit “give up” conditions to avoid getting stuck.
  • Geometry + constraints: clamp targets and always respect safety margins near boundaries.
  • Defensive coding: assume sensor values can be wrong and add sanity checks.

Our iteration loop

  1. Run a batch of sims and save the worst failures.
  2. Write down the exact failure mode in one sentence.
  3. Make the smallest change that prevents that failure.
  4. Re-run the same seeds to confirm it’s actually fixed.

The biggest improvement came from treating failures as “bugs” (reproducible, fixable), not as bad luck.

What I learned

  • Consistency is a feature you have to engineer.
  • Small guardrails (clamps, timeouts, fallbacks) can double your performance.
  • Debugging skills scale to everything: robotics, ML, and software.
